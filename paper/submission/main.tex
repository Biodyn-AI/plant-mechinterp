\begin{abstract}
Foundation models trained on plant DNA sequences are increasingly used in computational biology, yet their internal representations remain opaque. We present the first mechanistic interpretability study of a plant genomic foundation model, analyzing Plant-DnaGemma, a 152M-parameter Gemma-based transformer, using five complementary techniques with proper baselines and controls. Cross-validated linear probing reveals that genomic sequence type information peaks at middle layers (layer~7, 74.5\% accuracy), significantly exceeding both a randomly initialized model (45.8\%) and $k$-mer frequency baselines (67.0\%). An overcomplete sparse autoencoder (768$\to$3072 dictionary) achieves 71.0\% sparsity, decomposing representations into features with a heavy-tailed usage distribution consistent with findings from language model interpretability. Attention analysis across all 144 heads reveals a local-to-global processing hierarchy. Critically, cross-species analysis shows that the model's species discrimination (59.0\%) does not exceed a GC-content-only baseline (61.0\%), indicating that species-level representations primarily reflect nucleotide compositional differences rather than deeper phylogenetic knowledge. Our results demonstrate that plant DNA transformers learn representations significantly beyond simple sequence statistics for genomic region classification, but caution against over-interpreting species clustering as evidence of biological understanding. This work establishes a rigorous methodological framework for mechanistic interpretability of genomic foundation models.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Key message:} First rigorous mechanistic interpretability analysis of a plant genomic foundation model reveals learned representations beyond sequence statistics while showing species-level features primarily reflect compositional differences.

\section{Introduction}

Foundation models trained on DNA sequences have achieved strong performance across genomic prediction tasks \citep{dalla2023nucleotide, nguyen2024hyenadna, ji2021dnabert, zhou2023dnabert2}. In plant biology, models such as AgroNT \citep{mendoza2024agront}, PlantRNA-FM \citep{yu2024plantrna}, Plant-DnaGemma \citep{zhang2024plantdnagemma}, and Caduceus \citep{schiff2024caduceus} learn representations from raw nucleotide sequences across diverse plant species. However, the internal mechanisms by which these models represent genomic features remain poorly understood.

Mechanistic interpretability---which seeks to reverse-engineer neural network computations \citep{elhage2022toy, olah2020zoom, conmy2023towards}---has yielded insights in natural language processing, revealing interpretable circuits for tasks such as indirect object identification \citep{wang2023interpretability}, modular arithmetic \citep{neel2023grokking}, and factual recall \citep{meng2022locating}. In genomics, interpretability has largely focused on attention visualization and gradient-based attribution \citep{clauwaert2021explainability, avsec2021effective, yu2024plantrna}. No prior work has applied the full modern mechanistic interpretability toolkit to plant genomic models with proper baselines.

This gap matters. Plant genomes pose unique challenges: repetitive and transposable elements, diverse regulatory architectures, and wide variation in GC content across species \citep{schnable2009maize, arabidopsis2000analysis}. Understanding what plant foundation models learn about these features is essential for building trust and guiding improvements.

We present the first comprehensive mechanistic interpretability study of a plant genomic foundation model, analyzing Plant-DnaGemma (152M parameters, 12 transformer layers) with five complementary techniques and three baselines:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Linear probing} across all 13 layers with 5-fold cross-validation, compared against a randomly initialized model and $k$-mer frequency baselines.
    \item \textbf{Attention pattern analysis} of all 144 heads characterizing layer-wise specialization.
    \item \textbf{Activation patching} to assess causal dependence on regulatory motifs.
    \item \textbf{Overcomplete sparse autoencoders} (768$\to$3072) for feature decomposition.
    \item \textbf{Cross-species analysis} with GC-content and $k$-mer controls.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Related work}

DNABERT \citep{ji2021dnabert} adapted BERT-style pretraining for DNA; DNABERT-2 \citep{zhou2023dnabert2} replaced $k$-mers with BPE. The Nucleotide Transformer \citep{dalla2023nucleotide} scaled to 2.5B parameters. HyenaDNA \citep{nguyen2024hyenadna} demonstrated long-range modeling. Caduceus \citep{schiff2024caduceus} introduced bidirectional equivariant architectures. In plant biology, AgroNT \citep{mendoza2024agront} trained a 1B-parameter model on 48 plant genomes. Plant-DnaGemma \citep{zhang2024plantdnagemma} adapted Gemma for plant DNA. PlantRNA-FM \citep{yu2024plantrna} included surface-level attention visualization but no systematic mechanistic analysis.

Key interpretability techniques include linear probing \citep{alain2017understanding, belinkov2017neural, hewitt2019structural}, activation patching \citep{vig2020investigating, meng2022locating}, and sparse autoencoders \citep{cunningham2023sparse, bricken2023monosemanticity, templeton2024scaling}. Circuit analyses have revealed interpretable structures in language models \citep{olsson2022context, wang2023interpretability, geva2023dissecting}. Probing has been applied to protein language models \citep{vig2021bertology, rao2019evaluating, rives2021biological} but never systematically to plant DNA models. Our work bridges this gap.

\section{Materials and methods}

\subsection{Model and data}

We analyze \texttt{plant-dnagemma-BPE} \citep{zhang2024plantdnagemma}, a 152.4M-parameter Gemma-based transformer \citep{team2024gemma} with 12 layers, 12 heads per layer, 768-dimensional representations, and 8,002 BPE tokens. All experiments used a single NVIDIA RTX 2060 (6\,GB VRAM).

We generated 400 synthetic sequences (100 per class, 200\,nt) for sequence type classification (promoters, exons, introns, intergenic), 300 sequences (100 per species, 128\,nt) for cross-species analysis, and regulatory motif sequences for activation patching. See Online Resource~1 for generation details.

\subsection{Linear probing}

Hidden states from all 13 layers are mean-pooled to 768-dimensional vectors. $\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression ($C=1.0$) classifiers are evaluated via 5-fold stratified cross-validation reporting accuracy and macro F1 with standard deviations.

\subsection{Baselines}

\textbf{Random model}: Identical architecture with random weights. \textbf{$k$-mer frequencies}: $k \in \{3,4,5\}$ frequency vectors with logistic regression. \textbf{GC-content}: Single-feature classifier for species analysis.

\subsection{Attention analysis}

Attention weights extracted from all 144 heads with eager computation. Summary statistics and spatial patterns characterized per head.

\subsection{Activation patching}

Following \citet{meng2022locating}, regulatory motifs are corrupted and clean activations selectively restored to measure causal effects.

\subsection{Overcomplete sparse autoencoder}

768$\to$3072 dictionary (4$\times$ expansion), ReLU encoder, $\ell_1$ penalty ($\lambda = 5 \times 10^{-4}$), 200 epochs, Adam ($\text{lr}=10^{-3}$), standardized inputs. This follows the overcomplete dictionary learning paradigm of \citet{bricken2023monosemanticity}.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Trained model learns representations beyond composition}

Linear probing with 5-fold cross-validation reveals that Plant-DnaGemma builds representations significantly exceeding baseline approaches (Fig.~\ref{fig:probing}). The trained model achieves peak accuracy of \textbf{74.5\% $\pm$ 4.4\%} at layer~7 (chance = 25\%), while the randomly initialized model reaches only 52.5\% at its best layer (layer~0). This \textbf{+28.7 percentage point advantage} demonstrates that pretraining drives representation quality.

The trained model also outperforms $k$-mer frequency baselines: 3-mer (65.0\%), 4-mer (65.7\%), and 5-mer (67.0\%). The +7.5\% gap over the best $k$-mer baseline indicates that the model learns representations beyond simple sequence composition, though the moderate margin suggests a substantial compositional component.

\subsection{Layer-wise representational hierarchy}

The probing results reveal a hierarchy across layers (Table~\ref{tab:layerwise}). Accuracy increases from the embedding layer (70.0\%) through middle layers, peaks at layer~7 (74.5\%), then declines to 68.5\% at the final layer. The random model shows the inverse: best at the embedding layer (52.5\%), degrading through deeper layers, suggesting untrained layers corrupt input signals while trained layers refine them.

\subsection{Attention head specialization}

Analysis of all 144 attention heads reveals structured specialization (Fig.~\ref{fig:attention}). Early-layer heads exhibit diffuse attention; by layer~4, strong diagonal (local) patterns emerge for nearby-nucleotide attention. Middle-to-deep layers transition to sparse, distributed attention integrating information across the sequence. This local-to-global progression mirrors vision transformers \citep{dosovitskiy2021image}.

\subsection{Distributed motif encoding}

Activation patching on TATA and CAAT boxes reveals distributed representations. Individual motif corruption produces minimal activation changes, suggesting either distributed encoding of regulatory information---biologically plausible given combinatorial regulation \citep{lelli2012disentangling}---or insufficient corruption strength. We discuss this limitation below.

\subsection{Overcomplete sparse feature decomposition}

The overcomplete SAE (768$\to$3072, 4$\times$ expansion) achieves 71.0\% sparsity with ${\sim}$892 active features per sample (Fig.~\ref{fig:sae}). The feature usage distribution is heavy-tailed: few features are universally active while most are selective, consistent with language model SAEs \citep{bricken2023monosemanticity}. Reconstruction loss converges to 0.0083 on standardized inputs.

\subsection{Cross-species analysis: primarily compositional}

Model representations at the best layer (layer~8, 59.0\% $\pm$ 4.8\%) do \textbf{not} exceed the GC-content-only baseline (61.0\% $\pm$ 4.0\%) for species classification (Fig.~\ref{fig:species}). This indicates that species discrimination primarily reflects GC-content differences (36\% vs.\ 44\% vs.\ 47\%) rather than deeper phylogenetic knowledge---a cautionary finding for interpreting species clustering in genomic model representations.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{What pretraining learns.}
The +28.7 percentage point advantage over the random baseline demonstrates that pretraining imbues meaningful genomic representations. The +7.5\% improvement over $k$-mer baselines indicates higher-order pattern learning beyond composition, though the moderate gap suggests compositional statistics account for much of what the model ``knows'' about sequence types.

\paragraph{Layer-wise processing.}
The hierarchy---increasing accuracy through middle layers, then declining---broadly parallels language transformer findings \citep{tenney2019bert, rogers2020primer}, but with notable differences. Strong embedding-layer performance (70.0\%) reflects BPE tokenization informativeness, and the peak at layer~7 with gradual rather than sharp decline suggests less dramatic task-specific formatting.

\paragraph{The GC-content confound.}
Our cross-species results provide a methodological lesson: without the GC-content control, one might erroneously conclude the model learns phylogenetic relationships. While GC content is biologically meaningful, it cautions against over-interpreting species clustering as deep biological understanding. Future work should use real sequences with matched GC content.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1) Synthetic sequences with simplified characteristics; validation on annotated genomes would strengthen findings. (2) Single model analyzed (152M parameters); comparison with AgroNT limited by hardware. (3) Modest SAE dictionary (3,072 features); larger dictionaries may reveal finer features. (4) Activation patching null results may reflect insufficient corruption. (5) Cross-species sequences differ primarily in GC content.

\section{Conclusions}

We presented the first mechanistic interpretability study of a plant genomic foundation model with proper baselines and controls. Plant-DnaGemma learns representations that significantly exceed both random initialization (+28.7 pp) and $k$-mer frequency baselines (+7.5\%) for genomic region classification. Sparse autoencoder decomposition reveals structured feature usage consistent with language model findings. However, cross-species discrimination is largely explained by GC-content differences, cautioning against over-interpretation. Our framework---combining probing, attention analysis, activation patching, and sparse autoencoders with appropriate baselines---provides a template for rigorous interpretability analysis of genomic foundation models.
